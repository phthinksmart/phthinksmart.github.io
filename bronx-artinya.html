He then broke into a theatrical strut mimicking the Joker's dance down the stairs.Until recently not many tourists ventured into the Bronx, which has long sought to shake an unfair image as a crime-ridden backwater. The borough's biggest draws are the Bronx Zoo, the New York Botanical Gardens and the New York Yankees, the perennial baseball powerhouse that makes its home about a mile south of the steps, located on West 167th Street between Anderson and Shakespeare avenues.But since the release of "Joker" two weeks ago, many New Yorkers and out-of-towners traveled there to gawk, take selfies and, in the spirit of the Joker's choreography, to ham it up."There's something about his moves. He just killed it. It was in his soul," said Jay Garcia, a 30-year-old radio host from the neighboring borough of Queens.For longtime Bronx residents like Laura Harry, step streets are a common feature in the borough's hilly landscape."I've lived around here for 30 years ... people use them every day," said Harry, as she stood at the top of the steep stairs with her groceries, gazing at the people below.
